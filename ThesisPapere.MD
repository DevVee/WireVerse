1.1	 INTRODUCTION

The rapid evolution of technology requires electrical education to continuously adapt in order to prepare learners for increasingly complex electrical systems in order to prepare learners for increasingly complex systems. Traditional instructional methods—lectures, textbooks, and limited workshop access—often fail to cultivate both deep conceptual understanding and the hands-on proficiency necessary for real-world application. The gap between theory and practice results in learners being underconfident and unprepared to handle live circuits, safety protocols, and diagnostic challenges.
To address this gap, the study proposes the development of an interactive mobile application that integrates immersive 3D simulations for electrical training. The application virtually models tools, wiring systems, and circuit components to create a risk-free environment where learners can explore, experiment, and receive immediate feedback. Step-by-step tutorials and integrated assessments guide practical activities such as measuring voltages, assembling circuits, and identifying components, while reinforcing safety practices and procedural accuracy.
In addition to improving learning outcomes, the solution responds to broader educational and socio-technical needs. Institutions in under-resourced or remote areas gain access to a cost-effective alternative to fully equipped laboratories. Instructors benefit from a versatile teaching aid that complements classroom instruction and accommodates varied learning styles. The project also supports national workforce development goals by expanding access to skills training and strengthening foundational competencies required of aspiring electricians.





In summary, the proposed mobile application seeks to transform electrical education by making practical training more accessible, engaging, and effective. Through the integration of 3D simulation technology, this project aims to cultivate confident, competent learners who can seamlessly translate virtual practice into real-world capability.























1.2 BACKGROUND OF THE LOCAL
Pinoytek Skills Training and Assessment Center, Inc., located in San Fernando, Pampanga, is a TESDA-accredited institution that offers training and assessment in Electrical Installation and Maintenance NC II and related qualifications. As one of the accredited providers in Pampanga, Pinoytek plays a vital role in equipping learners with technical skills required for both local and overseas employment.
Despite its contributions, Pinoytek—like many technical-vocational institutions—faces challenges in maintaining consistent, safe, and cost-effective hands-on training opportunities. Limited resources, high equipment maintenance costs, and safety concerns in live workshops restrict the number of learners who can fully benefit from practical exposure. These limitations are particularly significant in programs like NC II and NC III, where learners must demonstrate competence in both theory and real-world applications to pass TESDA assessments.
With the increasing adoption of mobile technologies among students, introducing an interactive simulation-based system can provide Pinoytek and similar institutions with a flexible supplement to traditional learning. Learners can practice virtually, reinforce safety standards, and familiarize themselves with tools and procedures, even outside physical training labs. This aligns with TESDA’s goal of expanding access to skills development while ensuring that graduates are competent, confident, and job-ready.




1.3 Statement of the Problem

 1.3.1 GENERAL PROBLEM 
Learners in TESDA programs, particularly those enrolled in Electrical Installation and Maintenance NC II and NC III, often lack sufficient access to safe, affordable, and engaging training environments. Traditional workshop-based learning is limited by equipment availability, safety risks, and space constraints. As a result, learners may struggle to build the required skills and confidence before undergoing TESDA competency assessments.
This study seeks to address this issue by creating an interactive, mobile-based simulation system that supplements training and ensures learners gain enough preparation.
1.3.2 SPECIFIC PROBLEM
This research attempts to study the existing system with the aim of developing an 3D simulation to support Electrical Installation and Maintenance NC II and NC III training that specifically provide responses to the following problems:
1.3.2.1 How can a mobile 3D simulation application help students understand multi-step motor control procedures?

Students experience difficulty understanding multi-step motor control procedures, which affects their ability to follow proper electrical installation processes.
1.3.2.2 How can simulation improve learners’ ability to interpret electrical schematic diagrams and perform correct wiring connections?

Students struggle to connect schematic diagrams with actual wiring connections, resulting in frequent wiring errors.
1.3.2.3 How can a simulation-based system improve students’ confidence and readiness for electrical installation tasks?

Limited access to hands-on practice makes it difficult for learners to develop confidence and practical skills.





 













1.4 OBJECTIVE OF THE STUDIES

1.3.1 General Objective:
The general objective of this study is to develop an interactive mobile application that uses 3D simulations to support Electrical Installation and Maintenance NC II and NC III training, bridging the gap between theoretical knowledge and hands-on practice.

 1.3.2 Specific Objectives

The specific objectives of this study are the following:

1.3.2.1 To design interactive 3D simulation modules that break down motor control sequences into discrete, step-by-step tasks with real-time feedback, thereby reducing cognitive load and reinforcing sequential logic and safety procedures.

1.3.2.2 To integrate a schematic-to-3D wiring overlay feature that dynamically maps circuit diagrams onto virtual assemblies, improving learners’ accuracy in connections and troubleshooting proficiency.

1.3.2.3 To implement a graduated scaffolding framework within the mobile application that introduces circuits and motor control systems in increasing complexity, promoting gradual skill mastery and enhancing learner confidence.




1.5 THEORITICAL FRAMEWORK
 
WireVerse is supported by several learning and technology adoption theories that explain how simulation-based and mobile learning can improve technical skills training.

Constructivist Learning Theory states that learners gain knowledge through active interaction and experience. The proposed 3D simulation-based mobile application allows students to explore electrical components and practice wiring procedures in an interactive virtual environment, helping them build their understanding through hands-on learning.

Cognitive Load Theory explains that learning becomes more effective when complex information is presented in manageable steps. The system applies this principle by presenting electrical installation procedures through guided simulations and step-by-step instructions, allowing learners to better understand circuit connections and installation processes.

Experiential Learning Theory emphasizes learning through experience and practice. Since real laboratory equipment may be limited, the simulation provides a safe environment where students can perform virtual electrical tasks and gain practical experience before working with real electrical systems.

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) explains how users adopt new technologies based on perceived usefulness and ease of use. The mobile application is designed to be user-friendly and accessible, encouraging both students and instructors to integrate the system into electrical training activities.



1.5.1 Conceptual Framework:
Interactive mobile application that includes 3D models of electrical tools, wiring systems, and circuits, together with TESDA-aligned lessons and assessments. These serve as the foundation of the learning process. The process takes place when the app is used to explore simulations, follow guided lessons, and receive instant feedback on performance. 
Instructors may also demonstrate procedures, assign tasks, and monitor progress through the same platform. The expected output is stronger understanding, greater confidence, and enhanced competence in electrical installation and maintenance. With this cycle, the framework ensures better preparation for TESDA NC II and NC III assessments and more effective skill development.

1.7 SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS
	
	1.7.1 SCOPE
The 3D Simulation Mobile Platform for Electrical Installation Training and Maintenance focuses on the foundational aspects of electrical training. Coverage includes basic circuitry, wiring systems, and component identification through interactive 3D simulations, guided lessons, and assessment tools. The application is developed for Android devices and is intended for use in both classroom instruction and independent learning. Training modules follow TESDA competency standards to ensure relevance to national certification requirements.

1.7.2 LIMITATIONS

		The 3D Simulation Mobile Platform for Electrical Installation Training and Maintenance is limited to introductory and intermediate topics aligned with NC II and NC III qualifications, excluding advanced electrical engineering concepts. Performance may vary depending on the specifications of the mobile device used. Internet connectivity is required for system updates and monitoring features, which may pose challenges in areas with poor network access. Language support is restricted to English, and accessibility features for users with disabilities are not yet integrated. Graphics are optimized for smooth performance but may not fully replicate the complexity of real-world electrical systems. 






1.8 DEFINITION OF TERMS
	
To help readers understand the key concepts used in this study, the following terms are defined:

•	3D Simulation: A digital representation of real-world objects or systems in three dimensions, allowing users to interact with virtual environments as if they were real.

•	Assessment – The process of evaluating learner performance through activities, tasks, and quizzes to determine readiness for TESDA certification.


•	Circuit: A complete path through which electricity flows, usually consisting of wires, a power source, and electrical components like bulbs or switches.

•	Competency-Based Training – An approach to technical-vocational education that focuses on mastery of specific skills and knowledge required to meet national and industry standards.


•	Component: An individual electrical part, such as a resistor, capacitor, switch, or wire, that forms part of an electrical circuit.

•	Electrical Training: The process of learning about electrical concepts, components, systems, and safety procedures, usually for students or future electricians.


•	Interactive Learning: A learning approach that involves user participation through activities like clicking, dragging, or making decisions, often used to improve engagement and understanding.

•	Instructional Design – A structured approach to organizing lessons, simulations, and assessments to ensure effective and systematic learning.

•	Mobile Application (App): A software program designed to run on smartphones or tablets, used for performing specific tasks or learning activities.


•	TESDA NC II / NC III: National Certificates issued by the Technical Education and Skills Development Authority (TESDA) that certify competency in electrical installation and maintenance at Levels II and III.


 
1.9 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
		
The 3D Simulation Mobile Platform for Electrical Installation Training and Maintenance addresses the lack of accessible, safe, and consistent training resources in technical-vocational education. Many training centers, including Pinoytek Skills Training and Assessment Center, Inc. in San Fernando, Pampanga, face challenges related to limited equipment, high maintenance costs, and safety risks in live practice. These constraints often reduce opportunities for learners to gain sufficient exposure to practical tasks before undergoing TESDA competency assessments.
The project introduces a mobile application that integrates 3D simulations of electrical tools, wiring systems, and circuits. Learners use the application to complete guided lessons, perform virtual practice, and receive immediate feedback. Instructors apply the system to demonstrate procedures, assign activities, and monitor learner performance. The design follows TESDA competency-based standards to ensure alignment with NC II and NC III requirements.
Grounded in Constructivist Theory, Cognitive Load Theory, Experiential Learning, and the Technology Acceptance Model, the application promotes active participation, clear instruction, realistic practice, and ease of use. The framework combines inputs such as the mobile app and TESDA-aligned modules, processes involving instructional design and simulation-based practice, and outputs focused on improved learning outcomes and stronger readiness for assessment.
The system provides benefits to multiple stakeholders. Students gain confidence and competence through safe, flexible learning opportunities. Instructors receive a teaching aid that supports classroom and independent 


training. Training centers reduce reliance on costly facilities and equipment while expanding access to more learners. TESDA and government agencies advance workforce development by adopting inclusive, technology-based approaches. The electrical industry gains workers with stronger foundational skills prepared for both local and international employment.
The 3D Simulation Mobile Platform for Electrical Installation Training and Maintenance demonstrates how digital innovation can enhance technical-vocational education. By combining technology with competency-based training, the project strengthens learner preparation, supports institutional needs, and contributes to national skills development goals.
